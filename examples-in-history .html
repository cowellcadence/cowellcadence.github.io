Arizona's Unfinished Hippie Community 

Deep in the Arizona desert a small community lives. Many people tour the old village but attracts architects, they visit to tour the large half dome buildings and many other unique buliding designs. The main contributor to this project was Paolo Soleri. Soleri's image was to design a fully functioning, environmentally responsible city that could balance ecology and atchitecture. This unfinished utopia is called Arcosanti, it was officially opened in 1970. Unfortinutly, Arcosanti is barely 5% complete. The city was said to have fit a population of 5,000 people but only could fit 50 people. I don't believe that this city is a failure. There is still a community in Arcosanti and people still add on to Paolo's designs. As this city is still a community, it is yet to be finished. Soleri depended on thousands of people to aid him in this project but to his disapointment there was only a few. If there was a larger population that would have helped with this project I wouldn't doubt the sucess of Paolo Soleri's vision.  

Drop City Colorado

Drop City was established in 1965. The small hippie community was known for their dome shaped houses made out of recycled railroad ties and bottle caps as well as discarded lumber. At the time people young artist lived in this community. As more people lived in this small commute they made a joint bank account solely for food and supplies. This helped artists live off of their work so that they could focus on art instaed of getting a part time job. As the owners were younger they liked to do social experiments. The name Drop City originally came from an experiment where they would drop painted rocks from tall loft bulidings and watch the reaction of pedestrians. The name of the city was misinterpreted by many to be 'taking drops of acid'. The old owner confirms that the sole drug used in the community was coffee. The dome shaped buldings were inspired by the houses that the owners saw in Africa. Buckminster Fuller, a geodesic arhitect, was also a big inspiration for these buldings. He studied domes and was convinced that dome-like buldings were more durable. Unfortinutly, the owners of the plot lost many residents and had split from each other. The owners agreed to sell the land and went on with their lives.
